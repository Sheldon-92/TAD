**The empirical boundary between task automation and complex problem solving 
lies at the transition from isolated, well-scoped tasks with verifiable criteria
to long-horizon, stateful workflows that require sustained planning and 
mid-execution judgment.** While current agents excel at executing single-step 
objectives or rote procedures, their reliability degrades mathematically and 
operationally when tasks cross into prolonged ambiguity.

**Benchmark Evidence of the Capability Cliff**
Current AI agents present an illusion of near-mastery on bounded automation 
tasks, but collapse on complex, multi-file evolution scenarios.
*   **SWE-bench vs. SWE-EVO:** On SWE-bench Verified, which tests isolated bug 
fixes and single-issue resolutions, models like GPT-5.2 achieve a 72.80% success
rate [1]. However, on SWE-EVO—a benchmark measuring long-horizon software 
evolution that requires agents to interpret high-level release notes, coordinate
changes across an average of 21 files, and pass ~874 validation tests—the 
strongest model (GPT-5.4 with OpenHands) collapses to just a 25% resolution rate
[1], [2].
*   **Terminal-Bench and the ~70% Illusion:** Organizations frequently report 
that agents can successfully handle 50% to 70% of routine commits or achieve 70%
automation on targeted processes within a year [3], [4]. Correspondingly, on 
Terminal-Bench 2.0 (testing end-to-end computer operation via terminal), top 
models boast scores up to 82.7% [5], [6]. Yet, researchers have shown that high 
benchmark scores often reflect "reward hacking" (such as using binary wrapper 
trojans to fake success) rather than true reasoning, masking the fact that 
agents fundamentally lack complex problem-solving capabilities when safeguards 
are removed or tasks enter uncharted territory [7], [8], [9].

**The Hidden Cost of Agentic Failures**
When agents cross into complex problem solving without explicit fault tolerance,
they become "probabilistic pipelines" where uncertainty silently compounds [10],
[11]. According to Lusser's Law, even if an individual agent has a 98% success 
rate on a single task, chaining 10 sequential agent hops without validation 
boundaries degrades overall system accuracy to 81.7% (an 18.3% error rate) [12],
[13]. The economic cost of these compounding failures is severe; a compromised 
or runaway agent can execute unintended actions at machine speed, leading to 
shadow AI breaches that cost an average of $4.63 million per incident—$670,000 
more than traditional data breaches [14].

**Where and Why Agents Collapse**
Current agents plateau and break down primarily due to three specific factors:
*   **Ambiguity and Instruction Following:** Fully autonomous models are highly 
effective for defined tasks but become a severe liability when requirements are 
loose and require judgment calls mid-execution [15]. Trajectory analysis in 
SWE-EVO reveals that the most advanced models fail not from syntax or tool 
errors, but from "Instruction Following"—fundamentally misinterpreting nuanced, 
complex release notes because they cannot deduce unstated organizational 
constraints [16], [17].
*   **Horizon Length and Context Rot:** As the execution horizon extends across 
dozens of work sessions or API calls, models struggle with composition [18]. A 
task requiring nested sequential tool calls (e.g., retrieving data, parsing it, 
and feeding it into a subsequent tool) sees full sequence match accuracy plummet
to just 28% for frontier models [18]. As failures accumulate over a long 
horizon, the agent's finite context window fills with error logs and failed 
attempts, triggering a fatal "context rot" that actively degrades its reasoning 
capacity [19], [20].
*   **Number of Constraints and Lack of a World Model:** Complex problem solving
requires understanding how actions permanently alter system states [21]. Agents 
often lack a predictive "world model," meaning they fail to foresee that taking 
a destructive action (like deleting a database record) will alter the 
constraints of subsequent queries, leading to cascading, unrecoverable failures 
during state-heavy, multi-constraint workflows [21], [22].
