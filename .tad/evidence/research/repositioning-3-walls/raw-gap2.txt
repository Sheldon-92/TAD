workflows produce permanent, independent human skill growth** (where a human

However, there is strong evidence of **cross-domain capability transfer**. The 
Anthropic 2026 Agentic Coding Trends Report highlights a consistent pattern 
where **"everyone becomes more full-stack"** [1]. By augmenting their core 
expertise with AI agents, workers are successfully expanding into adjacent 
domains:
*   Security teams use agents to analyze unfamiliar code [1].
*   Research teams use them to build frontend data visualizations [1].
*   Non-technical employees use them to debug network issues or perform complex 
data analysis [1]. 
*   Engineers can now work effectively across frontend, backend, databases, and 
infrastructure where they previously lacked expertise because the AI fills the 
immediate knowledge gaps [2].

Regarding the risk of humans becoming **passive reviewers, experiencing skill 
atrophy, or over-relying** on agents, the sources present a mixed reality:

**Evidence of the "Passive Reviewer" Risk:**
*   **The shift to "editor":** Agentic workflows allow agents to work 
asynchronously and submit draft pull requests, structurally shifting the 
engineer's role from "writer" to "editor" [3]. 
*   **Review bottlenecks and diminished scrutiny:** As agents generate higher 
volumes of complex code, it creates severe review bottlenecks for humans. 
Coupled with intense pressure for development velocity, this dynamic actively 
reduces the level of security scrutiny humans apply to AI-generated output [4].
*   **Reliance on prior "hard way" learning:** The sources hint at the risk of 
foundational skill atrophy for new learners. One Anthropic engineer noted that 
effective delegation relies on pre-existing expertise: "I'm primarily using AI 
in cases where I know what the answer should be or should look like. I developed
that ability by doing software engineering 'the hard way'" [5]. 

**Counter-Evidence (Active Monitoring & Distrust over Blind Reliance):**
*   **Active Monitoring:** Analyzing millions of interactions, Anthropic found 
that experienced users do not simply abnegate oversight or blindly trust agents 
[6]. While experienced users auto-approve agent actions more frequently to let 
the agent run, they also **interrupt the agent more often** than novices [7]. 
This indicates a shift toward active monitoring, where users rely on "honed 
instincts" to step in and redirect the agent exactly when it goes off track [7].
*   **Expert Distrust:** Rather than experiencing over-reliance, experienced 
developers exhibit the highest rates of distrust toward AI accuracy [8]. A 
randomized controlled trial by METR found that experienced developers were 
actually **19% slower** when using AI coding tools because they spent 
significant time rigorously reviewing the output, ultimately rejecting more than
half of the AI's suggestions [8].
