**AI profoundly impacts reading comprehension through a dualistic mechanism 
defined as "Cognitive Augmentation vs. Cognitive Atrophy" [1].** On one hand, AI
can deeply enhance comprehension by acting as a personalized cognitive scaffold 
and research partner [1, 2]. On the other hand, relying on AI as a "solution 
engine" or an automated summarizer poses a severe risk of cognitive atrophy [1, 
3]. 

**How AI Flattens Comprehension**
Evidence shows that reading AI-generated summaries fundamentally flattens 
understanding compared to reading original source materials. An extensive study 
of over 10,000 participants found that while those using AI digests learned 
topics faster, they demonstrated shallower knowledge, remembered less, and 
offered fewer concrete facts [4]. Furthermore, advice synthesized by 
participants who relied on AI was shorter, less factual, and less trustworthy 
[4, 5]. 

This flattening occurs because AI summaries bypass the "desirable 
difficulties"—the cognitive struggles, trial-and-error, and mental heavy lifting
required to build robust, long-term mental models [3, 6, 7]. When learners 
outsource the deep reading process to an AI summarizer, they risk developing a 
"TL;DR" (Too Long; Didn't Read) mindset, which erodes sustained attention and 
inferential reasoning [8, 9]. Uncritical acceptance of AI outputs fosters 
intellectual passivity, substituting genuine critical thinking with an "illusion
of competence" [2, 3, 10].

**How AI Deepens Comprehension**
Conversely, AI deepens comprehension when used to demand more of the reader, 
rather than less. Studies in cognitive psychology demonstrate that active 
retrieval—forcing a learner to recall information actively—strengthens memory 
pathways and fosters deeper understanding through the "testing effect" [11]. 
When AI is utilized to facilitate this active recall and to adaptively space out
learning, it results in significant improvements in memory retention and learner
engagement [12-14]. 

### Concrete Design Choices for an AI Reading Companion

To ensure an AI reading companion increases active thinking rather than 
replacing it with passive summaries, designers must shift the focus from the 
final product (delivering answers) to the learning process [15]. Evidence 
supports the following concrete design choices:

**1. Implement AI-Generated Spaced Repetition and Flashcards**
Instead of providing a summary of a text, the AI should parse the reading 
material and automatically generate targeted question-and-answer pairs [16-18]. 
By utilizing spaced repetition algorithms, the companion can present these 
flashcards to the reader at expanding time intervals [19, 20]. Expanding the 
interval between tests makes information retrieval more difficult, forcing a 
deeper level of cognitive processing and cementing the facts in long-term memory
[20]. 

**2. Act as a Socratic Partner, Not an Oracle**
The AI companion should be designed to act as a "cognitive sparring partner" 
rather than an answer engine [2]. If a student highlights a complex concept, the
AI should be programmed to ask probing questions or ask the student to explain 
their reasoning, rather than merely defining the concept for them [21, 22]. For 
instance, an AI could generate counterarguments to a text's thesis, requiring 
the reader to deconstruct the AI's argument and formulate a robust rebuttal, 
directly exercising their critical thinking muscles [2, 9].

**3. Provide Robust, Low-Friction Active Reading Tools**
Active reading behaviors—such as highlighting and note-taking—are proven to 
improve knowledge gain [23]. A study on search-as-learning environments found 
that users who had access to highlighting tools covered 34% more subtopics in 
critical writing tasks, while those using note-taking tools covered 34% more 
facts [24, 25]. 
*   **Keep it integrated:** The companion must weave these tools directly into 
the reading interface. Switching contexts or grappling with complex system 
mechanics distracts the reader from the learning flow and increases cognitive 
load [26-28].
*   **Allow Multimedia Mapping:** For multimedia or nonlinear texts, provide 
mechanisms for learners to anchor their notes to specific timestamps or visual 
frames, as learners often struggle with the transient nature of audio-visual 
content [29-31].

**4. Scaffold the Synthesis Process**
To combat the flattening effect of immediate summaries, the AI companion should 
force the reader to do the synthesis themselves before offering AI refinement. 
The practical takeaway from AI summarization studies is that tools should be 
used to outline queries, compare viewpoints, or check citations *after* the user
has read multiple sources and attempted to write their own synthesis [1, 32]. 
The AI can then provide feedback on the user's synthesis, effectively treating 
the AI as a junior editor rather than an omniscient author [33]. 

By building systems that embrace "desirable difficulties" and prioritize 
Socratic inquiry, designers can ensure AI serves as a catalyst for cognitive 
growth rather than a crutch for cognitive decline [34].
